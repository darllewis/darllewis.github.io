    • Dissertation
      "Topics in Judicial Federalism,"
      The role and effect of federal structures on judicial outcomes is not well understood, despite the importance of such structures on legal outcomes in many countries. This project addresses these gaps in the literature in a series of essays on the role of federal structures in the development of efficient legal regimes, the outcomes that arise as a result of those institutions, and the place of the judiciary in a wider federal system.
    • Ongoing Projects
      "National Commerce, Local Interest, and a Federal Judiciary,"
      In the 19th Century, the focal point of the American judiciary was at the local level.  However, in an effort to prevent local actors from pandering to their constituencies at the expense of non-resident creditors, Whigs and Republicans in the Northeast helped to establish an expanded role for the national courts.  This paper develops a game-theoretic model of this phenomenon and places it in the context of the expansion of the Federal Judiciary during this period.    

      "Bias and Selective Expertise in State Courts,"
      In a recent series of papers, Matias Iaryczower and coauthors have developed a measure of judicial bias and quality.  This paper applies this model to state courts of last resort to determine the extent to which justices develop specific expertise in preferred issue areas while deferring to colleagues in other cases.  

      "Welfare and Federal Preemption of State Law," 
      Federal lawmakers must decide whether to assert federal authority over issues previously governed by individual states; however, when there are costs associated with legal change, individual legislators may be resistant to adopting uniform national standards, even when such standards are welfare-enhancing nation-wide.  This paper explores these effects in a game theoretic perspective.  

      "The Judicial Constraint,"
      There is a growing body of literature addressing the ability of the legislature to constrain judicial decisions.  This paper examines the argument from the opposite prospective, emphasizing the ability of the judiciary to leverage temporal constraints as a means of constraining legislative decisions.
